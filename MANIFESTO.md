We are angry because we have watched this happen before. We will watch it happen again — to every platform built on the same foundation — unless the foundation itself changes.

This is DEN. This is that different foundation.

## What Happened and Why It Keeps Happening

Every platform that has failed this community followed the same path.

They started permissive. They needed creators, so they welcomed us. The community came. Money followed. Then the platform needed institutional payment infrastructure to scale — Visa, Mastercard, PayPal — and the moment those processors came in, they brought a leash. Not a visible leash. Not a policy stated upfront. A leash that tightens gradually, one content category at a time, one terms of service update at a time, always citing "payment processor requirements" as if the platform had no choice.

They always had a choice. They chose growth over community. They chose institutional money over the people who built their platform. Every time.

This is not a problem of bad intentions. It is a problem of structure. When your ability to operate depends on the approval of Visa and Mastercard, you will eventually do what Visa and Mastercard require. Good intentions don't survive that architecture. They never have.

Payment processor capture is one attack vector. It is not the only one.

Legislators have learned the same trick. The EARN IT Act in the United States. The Online Safety Act in the United Kingdom. Chat Control proposals across the European Union. Each framed as child protection. Each functionally designed to pressure platforms into breaking encryption or building surveillance infrastructure that can then be expanded to cover whatever the next target is. The framing is the weapon — opposing it is made to sound like defending abuse. The actual target is end-to-end encryption and the privacy it provides. We have watched that pattern too. We are not naive about it.

The solution to both vectors is the same: a structure where neither leash can be attached in the first place. Not better intentions. Not stronger policies. A different architecture.

## What DEN Is

DEN is a protocol. Not a platform owned by a company. Not a service that can be sold, acquired, or pressured into compliance by a payment processor threatening to pull out. A protocol — like email, like the web itself — that any community member can run, fork, or build on.

The Decentralized Encrypted Network is built on principles that are not negotiable and not subject to change by any single person, company, or organization — including the people who built it.

**Crypto-native payments, fiat as a guest.** Your money moves through self-custodial wallets and smart contracts. Payments settle directly between subscriber and creator with no platform intermediary holding funds that can be frozen. The protocol is token-neutral — creators choose what token they price their work in, and the protocol does not recommend a financial instrument. Visa and Mastercard are not the main character here. They are guests, allowed into this ecosystem at the edges, with no structural power over what happens at its core. If a fiat processor leaves, the protocol continues. The kill switch they rely on does not exist in DEN.

**End-to-end encrypted content.** The instance operator storing your content cannot read it. The protocol cannot read it. A subpoena returns ciphertext. There is no scanning infrastructure to mandate, expand, or corrupt — because it does not exist. Decryption keys are governed by smart contracts: if you have an active subscription, the contract grants you access. If you don't, it doesn't. No platform logic, no human intermediary, no discretionary decision about who gets to see what. The math decides. This is not a policy position. It is an architectural fact, and it is intentional.

**Federated and forkable.** No single server. No single jurisdiction. Nodes run wherever operators choose to run them. If one instance is pressured, the network continues. If one instance goes dark entirely, that is a migration event — not a loss event. Your identity, your subscriber relationships, and your content references belong to you, not to any instance. No operator can hold your audience hostage. If our content policies don't suit a community, they can fork the protocol and run their own instance under a name that makes sense for them — [Name]DEN, their space, their rules, built on the same architecture. The code belongs to the community, not to any company.

**Governance that cannot be quietly changed.** Protocol rules are not a terms of service document that an executive can update at midnight. They are a public specification, ratified by the community, changeable only through a documented process that the community controls. When Collective Shout or any organization like it tries to pressure this protocol, there is no CEO to call. There is a public document. There is a community vote. There is a process. That process does not include them.

**Sustainable by design.** There is no central treasury. There is no investor with growth expectations to satisfy. Hosters — the denizens who run the infrastructure this protocol runs on — are compensated directly by the protocol based on the storage and bandwidth they provide, peer-to-peer via smart contract, with no platform intermediary. The protocol recognizes hosters as participants in the ecosystem, not vendors to be squeezed. The founding maintainer is sustained by voluntary community contribution, transparently, with no equity and no revenue share. Nothing in this protocol's economic structure creates pressure to compromise the community it serves.

## The Three Denizens

DEN is built around three types of participant. Every role in this protocol maps to one of them, and no role holds structural power over another's core participation.

**Hosters** provide the infrastructure. They run instances — the servers, storage, and bandwidth the protocol runs on. They are compensated for resources consumed, not for a cut of what creators earn. They set the community standards for their instance, above the protocol floor. They cannot read the content they store. They cannot hold a creator's audience or content captive. They are peers in the ecosystem, not gatekeepers.

**Creators** produce the work. They hold their own keys, their own identity, and their own subscriber relationships — independently of any instance they happen to be on. They earn directly via smart contract. They carry legal accountability for what they upload. They are portable: no hoster can take their audience from them.

**Subscribers** fund the ecosystem. Payment goes directly to creators via smart contract. Subscription state governs content access automatically — no platform decides who sees what. Subscribers are the only participants with plaintext access to content, which makes their voice the primary mechanism for flagging protocol violations.

Hosters serve creators and subscribers by providing infrastructure. Creators serve subscribers by producing work. Subscribers sustain creators by paying directly. The protocol governs the relationships between all three without sitting in the middle of any transaction.

## What Is Allowed Here

We will be explicit. Vagueness is the attack surface that organizations like Collective Shout exploit. Every edge case they can argue about is leverage. We remove that leverage by having clear answers to every question.

**Allowed:**
- Illustrated, 3D rendered, and written adult content between adult characters
- Feral content — fictional animals are not real animals
- Vore, gore, and taboo kink content — fiction is not endorsement, content warnings required
- Anthro and human characters — core to what this community creates
- Any kink or content type not explicitly excluded, with appropriate content warnings

**Not allowed:**
- Sexual content depicting minors — no jurisdiction carveout, no exceptions
- Photographic content of real human beings — this is a different legal and technical problem, it is not what DEN is designed for
- Content generated primarily by AI prompt — DEN exists to sustain human creative labor, not to host algorithmic output displacing the artists this community depends on
- Real person content without consent — defamation and privacy surface, full stop

**Instance level, not protocol level:**
Individual instances may add their own community standards above this floor. They may not go below it. The floor is the floor.

We are not going to soften any of these positions when someone applies pressure. The rules are written down. The reasoning is written down. If you disagree with a rule, the governance process is public. If the governance process doesn't give you what you want, the fork option is always available.

## On the CSAM Prohibition and Why We Don't Build Scanning Infrastructure

The prohibition on sexual content depicting minors is absolute. No jurisdiction carveout. No exceptions. This will not change.

We want to be precise about how this is enforced, because the answer is not what legislators pushing EARN IT, Chat Control, or the Online Safety Act want to hear.

DEN does not build scanning infrastructure. There is no hash-matching database. There is no client-side scanning. There is no PhotoDNA integration. There is no system that can be mandated, expanded by legislative pressure, or quietly updated to cover the next content category after minors. That system does not exist in this protocol, and its absence is intentional.

The encryption architecture means instance operators store ciphertext and cannot read what they host. The protocol has no access to plaintext. There is no backdoor to mandate because there is no backdoor. This is not a policy position about privacy preferences. It is an architectural fact. A subpoena to an instance operator returns encrypted data. Decryption keys are held by the subscriber and governed by smart contract — no platform intermediary holds them.

Legal liability for uploaded content sits with creators. The protocol is infrastructure, not a publisher. An instance operator who cannot read what they host is not the appropriate target of content enforcement, and the architecture makes that case clearly and structurally rather than asking anyone to take our word for it.

Subscribers — the only participants with plaintext access to content — are the reporting layer. A subscriber who encounters content violating the protocol floor has a reporting mechanism. That is how enforcement works: through accountability of the people who produce and access content, not through surveillance infrastructure that the people building this protocol could never be trusted to operate without abuse, and that would not stay limited to its stated purpose. It never has.

## On AI Content

The exclusion of AI-generated content is not arbitrary and it is worth explaining directly.

This community's economy runs on human artists. Commissions are how creators sustain themselves. The relationship between artist and patron in this community is one of the most direct and functional creative economies on the internet. AI content doesn't just pollute the feed — it undercuts that economy by flooding it with volume that no human artist can match on quantity, while matching none of them on the thing that actually matters: that a real person made this, for you, with skill they spent years developing.

DEN is explicitly pro-human-artist. The rule is: human creative labor must be the primary authorship of any content hosted here. Tools that assist human creativity are not the same as tools that replace it. The community will know the difference. Instance moderators will enforce the difference. The position will not change because a payment processor or an investor wants more content volume on the network.
